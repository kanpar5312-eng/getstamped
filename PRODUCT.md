# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

International F-1 visa applicants aged 17-25 (India, China, Vietnam, Nigeria, Brazil, South Korea, Bangladesh, Mexico, Iran, Saudi Arabia — ~500K applicants/year globally) and their parents, who are paying for the product on the student's behalf. Parents are often the ones evaluating trustworthiness and paying $30-100K for the underlying US education, so the interface has to read as credible and serious, not like a cheap side project.

## Product Purpose

GetStamped guides applicants step-by-step through the 47-step F-1 visa process (5 phases: Before I-20, After I-20, DS-160+fees, Interview prep, Post-approval), with AI Q&A, voice mock interviews, a document vault, and interview-day prep. One-time pricing (Basic free / Solo paid / Family for 2 seats). Success looks like a family trusting the product enough to pay for a one-time, non-refundable purchase tied to a high-stakes, high-anxiety process.

## Brand Personality

Editorial, serious, trustworthy — closer to The Economist, Aesop, or Apple's serious product pages than a generic SaaS tool. Calm confidence, not hype. Speaks to both an anxious 17-25 year old and a paying parent who wants reassurance, not gimmicks.

## Anti-references

No generic AI-SaaS look: no gradient text, no glassmorphism, no cheap/loud saturated orange used as a blanket accent, no hero-metric template, no identical card grids, no tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows, no numbered section markers as default scaffolding, no 3-tier pricing comparison table look-and-feel, no bouncy/spring motion. No blue, no purple, no neon, ever — confirmed twice as a hard rule. No overpromising copy ("guarantee", "ensures", "definitely catches").

## Design Principles

- Trust over hype: every visual choice should read as credible to a parent paying real money for their child's education, not as flashy marketing.
- Editorial restraint: hairline borders over soft shadows-as-default, generous whitespace, asymmetric layouts where earned — not decoration for its own sake.
- One deliberate accent, used sparingly: the warm saffron/persimmon accent marks CTAs and key moments, not every badge, tag, and button on a page.
- Real specificity: real numbers, real step counts, no placeholder vibes, no vague marketing language.
- Motion is rare and purposeful: reserved for a short list of approved moments (waitlist counter, form feedback, hero entrance, timeline reveal, CTA magnetic hover), never blanket fade-in-on-scroll or hover-scale everywhere.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

International, ESL-friendly audience — keep copy direct and simple. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` on every animated component (already enforced as a hard rule). Maintain WCAG AA contrast, especially body text against the bone/cream background and any text over the saffron accent.
